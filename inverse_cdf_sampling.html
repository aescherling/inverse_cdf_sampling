Inverse CDF sampling (inversion method)

The inversion method leverages the CDF of the (continuous) distribution you want to sample from in order to transform a pseudrorandom sample from the uniform (0,1) distribution to a sample from the desired distribution.

To understand how this works, first suppose that \(f(x)\) is some (continuous) density function of interest, \(F(x)\) is the corresponding CDF, \(X \sim f(x)\), and \(Y=F(X)\). Note that \(Y \in (0,1)\); each possible value of \(Y\) is a probability. Furthermore, the CDF of \(Y\) is

\begin{align} P(Y \le y) & = P(F(X) \le y) \\ &= P(X \le F^{-1}(y)) \\ & = F(F^{-1}(y)) \\ & = y. \end{align}

That is, if you transform a random variable by applying the CDF, you get a uniform distribution. The inversion method for sampling uses the same process in the opposite direction. Taking a uniformly (0,1) variable and applying the inverse CDF, we get the desired distribution. In particular, let \(U \sim unif (0,1) \), let \(f(x)\) and \(F(x)\) be the PDF and CDF for a desired distribution, and let \(Y=F^{-1}(U)\). It follows that:

\begin{align} P(Y \le y) & = P(F^{-1}(U) \le y) \\ & = P(U \le F(y)) \\ & = F(y). \end{align}

Hence, \(Y\) is distributed following the desired distribution.
